# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Aryan, a CS junior using this solo for his own cold-email campaigns to research professors. Power-use context: he knows the workflow, he's iterating fast, he's managing multiple batches at once. No onboarding needed; discoverability matters more than hand-holding.

## Product Purpose

OutlookScheduler is a personal campaign manager for professor cold outreach via Outlook on Mac. It batches, schedules, and tracks emails — and includes a professor-link verification workflow to catch AI-hallucinated recipient addresses before they go out. Success means emails land correctly, the user can review and verify without losing his place, and nothing gets sent to the wrong person.

## Brand Personality

Warm, precise, personal. Like Superhuman's speed and Loom's approachability — not a cold enterprise product, not a colorful marketing blaster. A well-made personal tool that respects the user's time and intelligence. Feels handmade: intentional choices, not template defaults.

## Anti-references

- Generic SaaS dashboard aesthetic: bland white canvas, blue left sidebar, identical card grid, uniform icon-label rows. This is a tool with a specific workflow, not a generic admin panel.
- Enterprise email client (Gmail/Outlook UI): grey toolbar soup, dense menu bars, no visual hierarchy.
- Mailchimp / bulk email sender: colorful, marketer-targeted, campaigns-as-the-product feel. This is personal outreach, not broadcast.

## Design Principles

1. **Personal over corporate.** This is one person's tool. It can have opinions, shortcuts, and a distinct feel. It doesn't need to look like it scales to 500 enterprise users.
2. **Workflow visibility.** The review-verify-schedule-send pipeline should be readable at a glance. The user should always know what needs attention and what can wait.
3. **Warmth without softness.** Approachable doesn't mean weak. Teal+orange palette, rounded corners, Inter — these carry warmth. The data density and precision carry weight. Both coexist.
4. **Recovery as a feature.** Duplicate warnings, professor-link verification, needs-review gating — these are intentional safety nets. Their UI should feel helpful, not alarm-y.
5. **No visual noise.** A one-person tool should be calm. Alerts only when something needs attention. Neutral states should feel quiet, not empty.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Solo use, no specific requirements stated. Target WCAG AA contrast (4.5:1 body text) as a baseline — easy to read matters for long review sessions. Status meaning should not rely on color alone (badge icons + text supplement color).
